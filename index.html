EXP 1 Minnesota Rate of Manipulation Test (M.R.M.T) ▾
Problem / Aim
To measure the manual dexterity of the subject by administering Minnesota Rate of Manipulation Test.

To achieve competence in activities requiring manual dexterity, speed of gross movements of hand and arm, manual rhythm and coordination are necessary. Gross movements of hand and arm may be measured in terms of the speed with which the individual picks up and places cylindrical blocks in holes on a board. Finger dexterity and coordination, necessary in speed and accurate manipulation of objects, may be tested by measuring the rate at which an individual is able to pick up, turn the block and replace it in the holes on the board.

Similar in concept to Stomberg dexterity test, which measures two operations: (a) Speed of fitting blocks into the holes; (b) Speed of turning over blocks already in the holes.
High score indicates true vocational aptitude for packing and wrapping. This test involves eye-hand coordination and to some extent two-hand coordination.
Placing series measures gross manual dexterity. Turning series measures both gross and fine dexterity and two-hand coordination.
Plan
Administer M.R.M.T to the subject. Conduct the experiment in two series — Placing and Turning. Obtained time scores are interpreted with reference to the standard norms.
Materials
M.R.M.T board — wooden board + wooden tray with 58 symmetrical holes 58 symmetrically cylindrical wooden blocks (Blue one side, Red other) Stop Clock Writing Materials
Precautions
  1. Demonstrate the task clearly before beginning.
  2. Give a practice trial before the actual test.
  3. Ensure the board is stable and at a comfortable height for the subject.
  4. Record time accurately using the stop clock.
Procedure
  1. Seat the subject comfortably in front of the M.R.M.T board.
  2. Placing Series: All 58 blocks are placed in the tray. Subject picks them up one by one and places them into the holes on the board as quickly as possible. Time taken is recorded in seconds.
  3. Turning Series: All 58 blocks are already in the holes. Subject picks each block up, turns it over, and replaces it in the hole. Time taken is recorded in seconds.
  4. The time score for each series is noted and compared to standard norms to obtain a percentile rank.
Instructions
Placing Series: "Pick up each block from the tray one at a time and place it into the holes on the board as quickly as you can. Start when I say 'Go'."
Turning Series: "Pick up each block from the hole, turn it over so the other colour faces up, and replace it back in the same hole as quickly as you can. Start when I say 'Go'."
Analysis & Norms
  1. Record the total time taken (in seconds) for each series separately.
  2. Compare the time scores to the standard norms table to obtain a Percentile Rank for each series.
  3. Interpret the percentile rank using the norms below.
PoorBelow 20

EXP 2 Competition on Performance ▾
Problem / Aim
To determine experimentally the effect of competition on the performance of the subject.

Competition is a very strong motive which promotes work and increases productivity without necessarily affecting the quality of performance.

Getting ahead of someone else, appears to be a basic human tendency. Competition begins in early childhood among siblings. An individual's performance is constantly compared with others and when one excels, the felt reward could be both intrinsic as well as extrinsic.

Most people respond to the challenge of competition with added effort and zeal. However, in some individuals the challenge of competition can have the opposite effect, due to anxiety.

Hypothesis
Performance of the subject significantly improves with competition.
Plan
To compare the performance of the individual subject under competitive and non-competitive conditions.
Variables
Variable TypeDescription
Independent VariableA set of instructions given to the subject to induce competition.
Relevant Variables1. Subjects are matched on variables of age, gender and writing ability.
2. Tasks are matched in terms of difficulty level and time allotted.
Dependent VariablePerformance of the subject under competitive and non-competitive conditions.
Materials
Data sheets with circles arranged in order Stop watch / Stop clock Writing materials
Precautions
  1. Avoid distractions during the experiment.
  2. Instructions to induce competitive spirit in the second series must be powerful enough to motivate the subject.
  3. Give a rest period of two minutes between the two series.
Procedure
  1. Non-competitive series: Seat the subject comfortably. Place the data sheet in front of the subject and demonstrate how to draw smiling faces in the circles. Give the instructions, start the stop watch simultaneously giving the 'start' signal. Allow three minutes. At the end of the time limit ask the subject to stop and count the number of faces correctly drawn. Give a rest period of two minutes before giving the competitive series.
  2. Competitive series: The same procedure is followed, but the knowledge of the scores of other subjects is given. The key factor in this series is the nature of instructions to the subject.
Instructions
Non-competitive series: "When I say 'start', start drawing smiling faces in the circles as shown earlier till I ask you to stop."
Competitive series: "You have been able to draw [mention number] smiling faces. There are other friends of your group who have been able to draw more faces than you. With a little more effort, I am sure you too will be able to draw as many or even more faces. Keep this in mind and try to excel. Start when I ask you to."
Analysis of Results
  1. Number of smiling faces correctly drawn in each series separately constitutes the score.
  2. Calculate the Competitive Index (C.I.) using the formula:
Competitive Index Formula
C.I. = Score in Competitive Series ÷ Score in Non-Competitive Series
C.I. is the competitive index — this would be more than 1.00 in case competition facilitates performance.

EXP 3 Size-Weight Illusion ▾
Problem / Aim
To study experimentally the effect of perception of size on judgement of weights.

Things that are big are usually heavier than things that are small (made out of the same material). If the weights of the bigger and smaller things are the same, we tend to judge the large thing as lighter. The experience of size gives us a wrong suggestion regarding the weight — an association formed by long experience of lifting various objects leads to this error in judgement.

This illusion is a normal phenomenon and has particular value as a measure of suggestibility in older children and adults. The larger the size of the block, the greater is the pressure applied on the block by the subject, and then finding it to be lighter than expected, the subject underestimates its weight. For the smaller sized block, less pressure is applied and finding it heavier than expected, the subject overestimates its weight.

Hypothesis
1. Perception of larger size leads to underestimation of weight.
2. Perception of smaller size leads to overestimation of weight.
Plan
To compare the extent of error committed in the three series while judging the weight.
Variables
Variable TypeDescription
Independent VariableThe size of the standard block.
Relevant VariablesThe progressive increase in the weight of the variable blocks and their identical appearance.
Dependent VariableThe judgement of weight given by the subject (Point of Subjective Equality).
Materials
Three standard blocks of different sizes weighing 55 gms each (one bigger, one equal, one smaller) Eleven variable/comparison blocks ranging from 30 gms to 80 gms (progressive increase of 5 gms, all equal size) Writing Materials
Precautions
  1. The subject is given no clue about the weight of the standard blocks.
  2. The random arrangement of the blocks should be done without the knowledge of the subject.
  3. Each block should be lifted with the preferred hand, with the same force and to the same height.
  4. The subject is required to keep sight on the block being lifted.
Mnemonic for precautions: No clue on wt. · random arr. · lift w pref. hand · sight
Procedure
  1. The table is arranged at such a height that subject's forearm will be parallel to the floor while lifting the weights. The eleven variable blocks are arranged in a semicircle, equidistant from each other. Variable blocks are kept in a random order of weight. The standard block is kept at the centre.
  2. The experiment is conducted in three series with two trials in each series.
  3. First Series (same size standard block): Subject lifts the standard block to a height of approximately 8 inches. Then lifts each comparison block to the same height in sequence (left to right). Subject indicates which one block out of 11 is equal in weight to the standard block. This is the Point of Subjective Equality (PSE).
  4. Second Series (bigger sized standard block): The bigger standard block is placed at the centre. Same procedure followed. Two trials given.
  5. Third Series (smaller sized standard block): The smaller standard block is placed at the centre. Same procedure followed. Two trials given.
  6. In the second trial of each series, the position of comparison blocks is interchanged without the subject's knowledge. The procedure is repeated. The weight of the block judged equal in each trial is noted separately and the average is calculated.
Instructions
"Here is a block (indicate the standard block) at the centre and also there are 11 blocks for comparison (indicate the variable blocks). Lift the standard block to a height of 8 inches and place it back. In a similar manner, lift the first variable block to your extreme left with the same force and to the same height. Then lift the block at the centre and then the second comparison block at your left. Thus lift all the eleven blocks comparing each time with the block at the centre. While lifting the blocks, keep your sight fixed on the block. You have to indicate one block out of the 11 blocks which is equal in weight to the block at the centre after lifting all the blocks. Lift the weights using your thumb, index finger and middle finger."
Analysis of Results
  1. The average of weights as judged by the subject on the 2 trials is the Point of Subjective Equality (PSE):
    Avg PSE = (Trial 1 + Trial 2) / 2
  2. In the first series, the difference between Avg PSE and the weight of the standard block (55 gms) constitutes the Constant Error (CE):
    CE = Avg PSE − Std. Wt. (55)
    (Constant error occurs due to innate inaccuracy in the individual's judgement)
  3. In the second and third series, the difference between PSE and the weight of the standard block constitutes the Gross Error (GE):
    GE = Avg PSE − Std. Wt. (55)
    (Gross error is a combination of innate inaccuracy in the subject AND inaccuracy in judgement because of size)
  4. The algebraic difference between Gross Error and Constant Error yields the Net Error (NE):
    NE = Gross Error − Constant Error
    (Net error is the error that occurs solely due to size)
Negative NE = Underestimation = Hypothesis 1 proved (bigger block → underestimates weight)
Positive NE = Overestimation = Hypothesis 2 proved (smaller block → overestimates weight)

EXP 4 Concept Achievement (Formation) Test ▾
Problem / Aim
To study the processes of Concept Achievement using the Concept Achievement Test by M. Rajamanickam.

Concept is defined as a categorisation of objects, events or people that share common properties. Words in any language are the symbolic representations of some objects, events or ideas. A symbol is an idea and it represents the object in the form of a word — a concept. Therefore, a concept is a word which has certain specific meaning and this meaning is universally accepted.

Concept achievement is the process of discovering some quality or property that are common to a series of objects. It refers to the description of some common element in objects and differentiating the quality of one object from another.

Three important psychological processes involved in concept achievement: abstraction, generalization, and discrimination.

Abstraction — Observing the features and characteristics of an object.
Discrimination — Finding the difference in the features between objects.
Generalization — Applying this rule to other similar features and objects.
Types of Concepts
TypeDescriptionExample
SimpleOnly one qualityColour white — white paper, white chalk
Conjunctive (Complex)Two or more qualitiesCat — 4 legs, a tail, animal, pet
Disjunctive (Complex)One quality puts it in the classFuel — wood, petrol, gas
RelationalCharacteristics vary comparativelyMore than, less than, rare, fatter, shorter
According to Heidbreder, concepts with concrete features are easier for concept attainment than concepts with abstract features and concepts with numbers.
The Concept Achievement Test by M. Rajamanickam is designed and developed for persons above 18 years.
Hypothesis
1. The number of correct answers progressively increases in each set for each category as concept achievement takes place.
2. Concepts with concrete features take less number of trials for concept attainment than object concepts, spatial concepts and number concepts.
Plan
The test is administered to the subject and the number of correct answers for each set is noted as also the category of concepts formed.
Variables
Variable TypeDescription
Independent VariableType of concepts — concrete, object, spatial and numerical.
Dependent VariableNumber of correct answers for each type of concept. Number of trials taken for concept attainment.
Materials

The Concept Achievement Test consists of:

  1. Response set — six sets of eight categories of pictures in different forms in each set with response words (non-sense syllables) at the bottom. The response words are the same for the six different forms of the same category of pictures.
  2. Stimulus set — Contains six sets of eight categories of pictures but without the response word at the bottom.
  3. Data Sheet
  4. Stop Clock
8 categories of pictures:
Concrete: Human Face (MUKR) · Animal (GALB) · Bird (LAGH)
Object: Jug (KEMG)
Spatial: Square Pattern (BIKT) · Circle Pattern (FARH)
Number: Three (CEVT) · Six (JISP)
Experimental Controls
  1. The order of presentation of the pictures of the response set to be changed for each set systematically.
  2. Each picture to be shown for 10 seconds and a break of 20 seconds to be given between each of the six sets.
  3. The experimenter should tell the subject the correct response word (four-letter non-sense syllable) only once for the first set of 8 pictures using the stimulus set.
  4. The correct response words are not to be given for the other sets.
  5. The subject should be given a 7th trial (with the 1st set) if they do not score even the minimum score of eight at the rate of one per category per set. This may be taken as the final level of concept achievement.
Procedure
  1. The experiment is conducted in six trials (each of the 6 sets = one trial). The experimenter prepares a data sheet, entering responses using 'R' for right and 'W' for wrong for each set.
  2. Each picture is exposed for 10 seconds. There can be 20 seconds time between sets.
  3. If the subject does not score the minimum score of 8 (one per concept per set), a seventh trial may be given with the first set.
Instructions
"I will show you a series of pictures. At the bottom of every picture, a four-letter non-sense syllable word is printed. This is called the response word. You will have to associate each picture with the response word. After this, I will show you the same picture in the same order but without the response word and you will have to tell me the response word of the picture."
Analysis of Results
  1. Assign a score of 1 for every correct response.
  2. Maximum score is 48. Minimum score is 0.
  3. Record scores in the data sheet for each set (trial) and each category.
  4. Calculate total correct responses and mean number of trials taken for each category type.

EXP 5 Wechsler Adult Performance Intelligence Scale (WAPIS) ▾
Problem / Aim
To assess the intelligence of the subject using WAPIS — Wechsler's Adult Performance Intelligence Scale.

According to David Wechsler (1975) — "Intelligence is the capacity to understand the world, think rationally and use resources effectively when faced with challenges."

The term 'Mental Tests' was first employed by Cattell in 1870. The modern intelligence testing movement was launched in 1905 by the French psychologist Alfred Binet. William Stern, a German psychologist devised the concept of the Intelligence Quotient (IQ).

IQ Formula
I.Q. = (Mental Age (MA) / Chronological Age (CA)) × 100
Since the time they were published in 1938, Wechsler Scales have proved to be the best available individual tests for evaluating intelligence among adults. WAPIS is the performance scale adapted to Indian conditions by Prabha Ramalingaswamy. It is an individual test, mainly used in clinical studies and in counselling.
WAIS consists of 11 sub-tests. The first six form the verbal scale and the last five form the performance scale. Only the 5 performance sub-tests have been adapted for Indian conditions as WAPIS.
Plan
To administer the five sub-tests of WAPIS to the subject and score and interpret the data with reference to the norms.
Materials
Booklet — Picture Completion items + Block Design designs Booklet — Picture Arrangement cards 16 red & white cubes (Block Design) 4 boxes with pieces (Object Assembly) Scoring key (Digit Symbol) Writing Materials Stop Clock Norms
Precautions
  1. Instructions must be simple and clear.
  2. In picture completion, the subject may not give a verbal response but will point to a missing part of the picture. The examiner should consider the item passed.
  3. Time limits should be followed strictly and where time bonuses are possible, record the subject's time on the items and credit the bonus correctly.
  4. Avoid errors in converting raw scores to scaled scores and scaled scores to IQs.
  5. The Block Design Test is discontinued after 3 consecutive failures. Failure on both trials of either design 1 or 2 is considered one failure.
Procedure — The 5 Sub-Tests
Subject is seated comfortably. After establishing rapport, the five sub-tests are administered in the following order:
  1. Picture Completion: 26 cards with pictures. An important part of each picture is missing. Subject must name the missing part. Time limit per item: 20 seconds. If subject cannot answer the first two items, examiner gives the answers, but no help is given for remaining items.
  2. Digit Symbol: Subject substitutes digits with symbols by referring to the key. Demonstrate the first 3 symbols, then let subject complete the remaining 7 items from the sample. Time limit: 90 seconds to complete the remaining 90 symbols.
  3. Block Design: Ten cards with designs. Four blocks (all red / all white / half-red-half-white) are given. Subject puts together the blocks to match each design. First two cards are demonstrated. Time limits: Designs 1–6 = 60 seconds each; Designs 7–10 = 120 seconds each. Discontinued after 3 consecutive failures.
  4. Picture Arrangement: 9 items made up of cards which when arranged tell a story. Cards are presented in the order indicated by the numbers on the back. Subject arranges them in a meaningful manner. If subject fails both items 1 and 2, discontinue. Time limit: 60 seconds for first 6 items; 120 seconds for items 7–9.
  5. Object Assembly: Pieces of each item are presented; subject arranges them to form an object. Order: Mannequin → Profile → Hand → Elephant. Time limit: first 2 items = 120 seconds; last two = 180 seconds.
Instructions
1. Picture Completion: "I am going to show you some pictures in which there is some important part missing. You have to name the missing part."
2. Digit Symbol: "Look at these boxes. Each has a number in the upper part and a mark/symbol in the lower part. Every number has a different symbol. By referring to this key, fill the remaining squares with respective symbols. Do this as fast as you can, when I give the ready signal. Do not omit any squares."
3. Block Design: "These blocks are all alike. On some sides they are all red, on some all white and on some half red and half white. I am going to put them together to make a design. Similarly you have to arrange the blocks to match the design on a card. There are 10 cards, work fast."
4. Picture Arrangement: "These pictures tell a story about a bird building a nest, but they are in wrong order. Put them in the right order so that they will tell a story. There are altogether 9 sets of picture, which you have to put in order. Work fast."
5. Object Assembly: "If these pieces are put together correctly, they will make something. Go ahead and put them together as quickly as you can."

EXP 1 MRMT — Key Concepts ▾
What is Manual Dexterity?

To achieve competence in activities requiring manual dexterity, speed of gross movements of hand and arm, manual rhythm and coordination are necessary. Gross movements of hand and arm are measured in terms of the speed with which the individual picks up and places cylindrical blocks in holes on a board. Finger dexterity and coordination are measured by the rate at which the individual picks up, turns the block and replaces it in the holes.

MRMT is similar to Stomberg dexterity test — measures: (a) Speed of fitting blocks into the holes; (b) Speed of turning over blocks already in the holes.
It is observed from a study on wrappers and packers that these two groups scored higher than the general population on the MRMT. High score = true vocational aptitude for packing and wrapping. The test involves eye-hand coordination and to some extent two-hand coordination.
Placing series = gross manual dexterity. Turning series = both gross and fine dexterity + two-hand coordination.
Instructions
Placing Series: "Pick up each block from the tray one at a time and place it into the holes on the board as quickly as you can. Start when I say 'Go'."
Turning Series: "Pick up each block from the hole, turn it over so the other colour faces up, and replace it back in the same hole as quickly as you can. Start when I say 'Go'."

EXP 3 Size-Weight Illusion — Key Concepts ▾
What is Suggestion?

Stimulus conditions which lead to uncritical conformity is known as suggestion — involves the deliberate manipulation of one's behaviour by another, often the former being unaware of this manipulation. Past conditioning and learning of the person makes him conform.

Suggestion refers to the stimulus condition that produces conformity. Suggestibility denotes an individual's predisposition to confirm or accept suggestion. One such laboratory-induced condition is ideo-motor suggestion.

The Illusion

Things that are big are usually heavier than things that are small (made out of the same material). If the weights of the bigger and smaller things are the same, we tend to judge the large thing as lighter. This illusion is a normal phenomenon and has particular value as a measure of suggestibility in older children and adults.

Larger block → greater pressure applied → found lighter than expected → subject underestimates its weight.
Smaller block → less pressure applied → found heavier than expected → subject overestimates its weight.

Viva Q&A
What is the difference between Constant Error, Gross Error, and Net Error? EXP 3
CE (Series I) = Avg PSE − 55. It is due to innate inaccuracy in judgement. GE (Series II & III) = Avg PSE − 55. It combines innate inaccuracy AND error due to size. NE = GE − CE. It is the error that occurs solely due to the size of the block.
What does a negative Net Error mean? EXP 3
A negative Net Error means underestimation — the subject judged the bigger block as lighter than it actually is. This proves Hypothesis 1.

Viva Q&A
Who developed WAPIS and what is it? EXP 5
WAPIS (Wechsler Adult Performance Intelligence Scale) is the performance scale of WAIS adapted to Indian conditions by Prabha Ramalingaswamy. It consists of 5 sub-tests: Picture Completion, Digit Symbol, Block Design, Picture Arrangement, and Object Assembly. It is an individual test used in clinical studies and counselling.
When is the Block Design test discontinued? EXP 5
The Block Design test is discontinued after 3 consecutive failures. Failure on both trials of either design 1 or 2 is considered one failure.
What are the IQ classification categories? EXP 5
Based on scaled scores and IQ conversion, classifications typically include: Average, Above Average, Superior, Very Superior. Refer to the WAPIS norms table for exact ranges.
STATS The Median Test ▾
What it is

The Median Test is used to compare the performance of two independent groups. A common median is found. If the two groups have been drawn at random from the same population, 1/2 of the scores in each group should lie above and 1/2 below the common median. To test this null hypothesis, we draw up a 2 × 2 table and calculate X² (Chi-square).

Above vs. below the common median constitutes one category in this table; experimental group vs. control group, the other.

Steps to Calculate
  1. Combine all scores from both groups and arrange in ascending order.
  2. Calculate the Common Median using: Mdn = (N + 1) / 2
  3. For each group, count how many scores fall above the common median and how many fall below.
  4. Enter the frequencies into a 2 × 2 fold table:

                    Below Median     Above Median     Total
    Exp. Group     A                C                  (A+C)
    Control Group   B                D                  (B+D)
    Total              (A+B)            (C+D)              N
  5. Calculate X² using the formula below.
  6. Calculate df = (r-1)(c-1) = (2-1)(2-1) = 1 for a 2×2 table.
  7. Enter Table E with df = 1. At 0.05 level = 3.84; at 0.01 level = 6.63.
  8. Interpret: if obtained X² > table value, null hypothesis is rejected.
Formula
Chi-Square for Median Test
X² = N (|AD − BC|)² / (A+B)(C+D)(A+C)(B+D)
Yate's Correction is used when any one of the box entries is 5 or less than 5:
X² = N (|AD − BC| − N/2)² / (A+B)(C+D)(A+C)(B+D)
Interpretation
If obtained X² > table value → Null hypothesis rejected. There is a significant difference between the two groups.
If obtained X² < table value → Null hypothesis retained. No significant difference between groups.
df = 1 → Table values: 0.05 level = 3.84  ·  0.01 level = 6.63
Worked Example

A clinical psychologist wants to investigate the effects of a tranquilizer on hand tremor. 14 patients are given the drug (experimental group) and 14 patients are given the Placebo (control group). Tremor is measured by a steadiness tester. Does the drug increase steadiness (indicated by a lower score in the experimental group)?

N = 28 total scores. Mdn = (28+1)/2 = 14.5
Arrange in ascending order → 14th score = 52, 15th score = 54 → Common Median = 53
