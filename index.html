EXP 1 Minnesota Rate of Manipulation Test (M.R.M.T) ▾
Problem / Aim
To measure the manual dexterity of the subject by administering Minnesota Rate of Manipulation Test.

To achieve competence in activities requiring manual dexterity, speed of gross movements of hand and arm, manual rhythm and coordination are necessary. Gross movements of hand and arm may be measured in terms of the speed with which the individual picks up and places cylindrical blocks in holes on a board. Finger dexterity and coordination, necessary in speed and accurate manipulation of objects, may be tested by measuring the rate at which an individual is able to pick up, turn the block and replace it in the holes on the board.

Similar in concept to Stomberg dexterity test, which measures two operations: (a) Speed of fitting blocks into the holes; (b) Speed of turning over blocks already in the holes.
Observed from a study on wrappers and packers — these two groups scored higher than the general population on MRMT. High score indicates a true vocational aptitude for packing and wrapping. The test involves eye-hand coordination and to some extent two-hand coordination.
Placing series measures gross manual dexterity. Turning series measures both gross and fine dexterity and two-hand coordination.
Plan
Administer M.R.M.T to the subject. Conduct the experiment in two series — Placing and Turning. Obtained time scores are interpreted with reference to the standard norms.
Materials
M.R.M.T board — wooden board + wooden tray with 58 symmetrical holes 58 symmetrical cylindrical wooden blocks — Blue one side, Red the other Stop Clock Writing Materials
Procedure
  1. Set-up (Placing Series): Place the board with red blocks atop. Slide the board to the desired position and lift, leaving the blocks on the table. Keep the tray back on the board. The subject stands at the centre of the table. The experimenter demonstrates the task with proper demonstration.
  2. Placing Series — Right-handed subject: Subject uses the preferred (right) hand, picks up the block nearest to her, and places it in the nearest hole in the left-hand column of the board. Takes the next block in the same column and places it in the next hole. When the first column is over, moves to the next column — continuing till all holes are filled. (Left-handed subjects begin from the right end of the board.) Give the 'start' signal simultaneously starting the stop clock. When the subject completes the board, stop the clock and note the time. Give four more trials. First trial = practice trial. Next four trials are used for scoring and analysis.
  3. Set-up (Turning Series): The experimenter leaves the blocks in the board with red atop. Proceed column-wise from bottom to top.
  4. Turning Series — Right-handed subject: Subject picks up the block nearest to her on the left side of the column with the right hand, twists the wrist until the blue side faces the left hand, grasps it in the left hand, and places it back in the same hole in the first column on the left side. Proceed column-wise from bottom to top. Throughout the experiment the subject stands at the centre of the table. The experimenter demonstrates the procedure. Give 'start' signal, simultaneously start the stop clock. Note the time. Four more trials given; first trial is practice. Scoring and analysis is done for the next 4 trials.
Instructions
Placing Series (to Right-handed subject):
"This is a speed test. You have to place all the blocks into the holes of the board as fast as you can. Use one hand. Reach directly to the block nearest to you in the left hand column. Pick it up and put it in the nearest hole in the board, in the left hand column of holes. Then take the next block in same column and put it in the next hole in the same column. When you complete the first column, go to the next column. Follow the same procedure till you fill up all the holes. Always stand at the centre of the table."
Turning Series (to Right-handed subject):
"You have to pick up the block from the hole with right hand, until the blue side is facing toward the left hand and grasp it in the left hand, and put it back in the same hole blue atop. Start from the left side with the block nearest to you. Proceed column wise towards the top. Continue the same procedure for all the blocks."
Precautions
  1. Demonstration should be given in both placing and turning series.
  2. Subject should stand only at the centre of the table and is not allowed to move either way.
  3. Time should be recorded accurately.
Analysis of Data
  1. Consider the time taken in the last four trials for analysis. First trial is a practice trial.
  2. Record the total of placing and turning series separately.
  3. Refer total time score in seconds and locate the percentile point in the norms. Lower time = faster = better performance.

EXP 2 Competition on Performance ▾
Problem / Aim
To determine experimentally the effect of competition on the performance of the subject.

Competition is a very strong motive which promotes work and increases productivity without necessarily affecting the quality of performance.

Getting ahead of someone else, appears to be a basic human tendency. Competition begins in early childhood among siblings. An individual's performance is constantly compared with others and when one excels, the felt reward could be both intrinsic as well as extrinsic.

Most people respond to the challenge of competition with added effort and zeal. However, in some individuals the challenge of competition can have the opposite effect, due to anxiety.

Hypothesis
Performance of the subject significantly improves with competition.
Plan
To compare the performance of the individual subject under competitive and non-competitive conditions.
Variables
Variable TypeDescription
Independent VariableA set of instructions given to the subject to induce competition.
Relevant VariablesTasks are matched in terms of difficulty level and time allotted.
Dependent VariablePerformance of the subject under competitive and non-competitive conditions.
Materials
Data sheets with circles arranged in order Stop watch / Stop clock Writing materials 🍫 Chocolate (reward)
Precautions
  1. Avoid distractions during the experiment.
  2. Instructions to induce competitive spirit in the second series must be powerful enough to motivate the subject.
  3. Give a rest period between the two series.
Procedure
  1. Non-competitive series: Seat the subject comfortably. Place the data sheet in front of the subject and demonstrate how to draw smiling faces in the circles. Give the instructions, start the stop watch simultaneously giving the 'start' signal. Allow three minutes. At the end of the time limit ask the subject to stop and count the number of faces correctly drawn. Give a rest period before giving the competitive series.
  2. Competitive series: The same procedure is followed. The key factor in this series is the nature of instructions — the subject is told they can win a chocolate reward if they beat their own previous score. Start the stop watch simultaneously giving the 'start' signal. Allow three minutes.
Instructions
Non-competitive series:
"When I say 'start', start drawing smiling faces in the circles as shown earlier till I ask you to stop."
🍫 Competitive series (Chocolate incentive):
"You drew [mention score] smiling faces just now — that's a great start! I have a challenge for you: if you can beat your own score this time, you win a chocolate. I know you can do better — you've already shown me what you're capable of. Give it everything you've got. Ready? Start!"
Analysis of Results
  1. Number of smiling faces correctly drawn in each series separately constitutes the score.
  2. Calculate the Competitive Index (C.I.):
Competitive Index Formula
C.I. = Score in Competitive Series Score in Non-Competitive Series
C.I. is the competitive index — this would be more than 1.00 if competition facilitates performance.

EXP 3 Size-Weight Illusion ▾
Problem / Aim
To study experimentally the effect of perception of size on judgement of weights.

Stimulus conditions which lead to uncritical conformity is known as suggestion — it involves the deliberate manipulation of one's behaviour by another, often the former being unaware. Past conditioning and learning makes the person conform. Suggestibility denotes an individual's predisposition to confirm or accept suggestion. One such laboratory-induced condition is ideo-motor suggestion.

Things that are big are usually heavier than things that are small. If both weigh the same, we judge the larger one as lighter. This illusion is a normal phenomenon and has particular value as a measure of suggestibility in older children and adults. The larger the size of the block, the greater the pressure applied, and finding it lighter than expected, the subject underestimates its weight. For the smaller block, less pressure is applied and finding it heavier, the subject overestimates its weight.

Hypothesis
1. Perception of larger size leads to underestimation of weight.
2. Perception of smaller size leads to overestimation of weight.
Plan
To compare the extent of error committed in the three series while judging the weight.
Variables
Variable TypeDescription
Independent VariableThe size of the standard block.
Relevant VariablesThe progressive increase in the weight of the variable blocks and their identical appearance.
Dependent VariableThe judgement of weight given by the subject (Point of Subjective Equality).
Materials
Three standard blocks of different sizes weighing 55 gms each (one bigger, one equal, one smaller) Eleven variable/comparison blocks ranging from 30 gms to 80 gms (progressive increase of 5 gms, all equal size) Writing Materials
Precautions
  1. The subject is given no clue about the weight of the standard blocks.
  2. The random arrangement of the blocks should be done without the knowledge of the subject.
  3. Each block should be lifted with the preferred hand, with the same force and to the same height.
  4. The subject is required to keep sight on the block being lifted.
Procedure
  1. The table is arranged at such a height that the subject's forearm will be parallel to the floor while lifting. The eleven variable blocks are arranged in a semicircle, equidistant from each other. Variable blocks are in a random order of weight. The standard block is kept at the centre. The subject is asked to stand and use the preferred hand to lift the weights.
  2. The experiment is conducted in three series with two trials in each series.
  3. First Series (same size standard block): Subject lifts the standard block to a height of approximately 8 inches and places it back. Then lifts each comparison block (left to right) to the same height, comparing each time with the standard. Subject indicates which one block out of 11 is equal in weight to the standard — this is the Point of Subjective Equality (PSE).
  4. Second Series (bigger sized standard block): Bigger standard block placed at the centre. Same procedure. Two trials given.
  5. Third Series (smaller sized standard block): Smaller standard block placed at the centre. Same procedure. Two trials given.
  6. In the second trial of each series, the position of comparison blocks is interchanged without the subject's knowledge. The weight of the block judged equal in each trial is noted separately and the average is calculated.
Instructions
"Here is a block (indicate the standard block) at the centre and also there are 11 blocks for comparison (indicate the variable blocks). Lift the standard block to a height of 8 inches and place it back. In a similar manner, lift the first variable block to your extreme left with the same force and to the same height. Then lift the block at the centre and then the second comparison block at your left. Thus lift all the eleven blocks comparing each time with the block at the centre. While lifting the blocks, keep your sight fixed on the block. You have to indicate one block out of the 11 blocks which is equal in weight to the block at the centre after lifting all the blocks. Lift the weights using your thumb, index finger and middle finger."
Analysis of Results
  1. The average of weights judged on the 2 trials is the PSE:
    Avg PSE = Trial 1 + Trial 2 2
  2. In Series I, Constant Error (CE): CE = Avg PSE − 55
    (Due to innate inaccuracy in the individual's judgement)
  3. In Series II & III, Gross Error (GE): GE = Avg PSE − 55
    (Combination of innate inaccuracy AND inaccuracy due to size)
  4. Net Error (NE) in Series II & III: NE = GE − CE
    (Error that occurs solely due to size)
Negative NE = Underestimation → Hypothesis 1 proved (bigger block)
Positive NE = Overestimation → Hypothesis 2 proved (smaller block)

EXP 4 Concept Achievement (Formation) Test ▾
Problem / Aim
To study the processes of Concept Achievement using the Concept Achievement Test by M. Rajamanickam.

Concept is defined as a categorisation of objects, events or people that share common properties. Words in any language are the symbolic representations of some objects, events or ideas. A symbol is an idea and it represents the object in the form of a word — a concept. Therefore, a concept is a word which has certain specific meaning and this meaning is universally accepted.

Concept achievement is the process of discovering some quality or property common to a series of objects. Three important psychological processes: abstraction, generalization, and discrimination.

Abstraction — Observing the features and characteristics of an object.
Discrimination — Finding the difference in the features between objects.
Generalization — Applying this rule to other similar features and objects.
Types of Concepts
TypeDescriptionExample
SimpleOnly one qualityColour white — white paper, white chalk
Conjunctive (Complex)Two or more qualitiesCat — 4 legs, a tail, animal, pet
Disjunctive (Complex)One quality puts it in the classFuel — wood, petrol, gas
RelationalCharacteristics vary comparativelyMore than, less than, rare, fatter, shorter
According to Heidbreder, concepts with concrete features are easier for concept attainment than concepts with abstract features and concepts with numbers.
The Concept Achievement Test by M. Rajamanickam is designed and developed for persons above 18 years.

EXP 5 Wechsler Adult Performance Intelligence Scale (WAPIS) ▾
Problem / Aim
To assess the intelligence of the subject using WAPIS — Wechsler's Adult Performance Intelligence Scale.

According to David Wechsler (1975) — "Intelligence is the capacity to understand the world, think rationally and use resources effectively when faced with challenges."

The term 'Mental Tests' was first employed by Cattell in 1870. The modern intelligence testing movement was launched in 1905 by the French psychologist Alfred Binet. William Stern, a German psychologist devised the concept of the Intelligence Quotient (IQ).

IQ Formula
I.Q. = Mental Age (MA) Chronological Age (CA) × 100
Since 1938, Wechsler Scales have proved to be the best available individual tests for evaluating intelligence among adults. WAPIS is the performance scale adapted to Indian conditions by Prabha Ramalingaswamy. It is an individual test, mainly used in clinical studies and in counselling.
WAIS consists of 11 sub-tests. The first six form the verbal scale and the last five form the performance scale. Only the 5 performance sub-tests have been adapted for Indian conditions as WAPIS.
Plan
To administer the five sub-tests of WAPIS to the subject and score and interpret the data with reference to the norms.
Materials
Booklet — Picture Completion items + Block Design designs Booklet — Picture Arrangement cards 16 red & white cubes (Block Design) 4 boxes with pieces (Object Assembly) Scoring key (Digit Symbol) Writing Materials Stop Clock Norms
Precautions
  1. Instructions must be simple and clear.
  2. In Picture Completion, the subject may not give a verbal response but will point to the missing part. The examiner should consider the item passed.
  3. Time limits should be followed strictly and where time bonuses are possible, record the subject's time and credit the bonus correctly.
  4. Avoid errors in converting raw scores to scaled scores and scaled scores to IQs.
  5. The Block Design Test is discontinued after 3 consecutive failures. Failure on both trials of either design 1 or 2 is considered one failure.
Procedure — The 5 Sub-Tests
Subject is seated comfortably. After establishing rapport, the five sub-tests are administered in the following order:
  1. Picture Completion — Analyses: Perceptual alertness and ability to distinguish essential from non-essential.
    26 cards with pictures. An important part of each picture is missing. Subject must name the missing part. Time limit per item: 20 seconds. If subject cannot answer the first two items, examiner gives the answers, but no help is given for remaining items.
  2. Digit Symbol — Analyses: Visual-motor speed.
    Subject substitutes digits with symbols by referring to the key. Demonstrate the first 3 symbols, then let subject complete the remaining 7 items from the sample. Time limit: 90 seconds to complete the remaining 90 symbols.
  3. Block Design — Analyses: Concept formation, visual motor coordination, spatial organization.
    Ten cards with designs. Four blocks (all red / all white / half-red-half-white) are given. Subject puts together the blocks to match each design. First two cards are demonstrated. Time limits: Designs 1–6 = 60 seconds each; Designs 7–10 = 120 seconds each. Discontinued after 3 consecutive failures.
  4. Picture Arrangement — Analyses: Measure of social intelligence; ability to comprehend total situation, evaluate implications of actions, and interpret/anticipate social events.
    9 items made up of cards which when arranged tell a story. Cards presented in order indicated by the numbers on the back. Subject arranges them in a meaningful manner. If subject fails both items 1 and 2, discontinue. Two trials given for first 2 items. Time limit: 60 seconds for first 6 items; 120 seconds for items 7–9.
  5. Object Assembly — Analyses: Visual motor coordination, perceptual speed and manipulation.
    Pieces of each item are presented; subject arranges them to form an object. Order: Mannequin → Profile → Hand → Elephant. Time limit: first 2 items = 120 seconds; last two = 180 seconds.
Instructions
1. Picture Completion: "I am going to show you some pictures in which there is some important part missing. You have to name the missing part."
2. Digit Symbol: "Look at these boxes. Each has a number in the upper part and a mark/symbol in the lower part. Every number has a different symbol. By referring to this key, fill the remaining squares with respective symbols. Do this as fast as you can, when I give the ready signal. Do not omit any squares."
3. Block Design: "These blocks are all alike. On some sides they are all red, on some all white and on some half red and half white. I am going to put them together to make a design. Similarly you have to arrange the blocks to match the design on a card. There are 10 cards, work fast."
4. Picture Arrangement: "These pictures tell a story about a bird building a nest, but they are in wrong order. Put them in the right order so that they will tell a story. There are altogether 9 sets of pictures which you have to put in order. Work fast."
5. Object Assembly: "If these pieces are put together correctly, they will make something. Go ahead and put them together as quickly as you can."

What is Suggestion?

Stimulus conditions which lead to uncritical conformity is known as suggestion. Suggestibility denotes an individual's predisposition to confirm or accept suggestion. One such laboratory-induced condition is ideo-motor suggestion.

The Illusion
Larger block → greater pressure applied → found lighter than expected → subject underestimates its weight.
Smaller block → less pressure applied → found heavier than expected → subject overestimates its weight.
Key Error Terms

Difference between CE, GE, and NE? EXP 3

Negative NE = underestimation — the subject judged the bigger block as lighter than it actually is. This proves Hypothesis 1.

Simple (one quality), Conjunctive (two or more), Disjunctive (one quality puts it in the class), Relational (characteristics vary comparatively).
What happens if subject fails to score the minimum in 6 sets? EXP 4
A 7th trial is given using the 1st set — this is taken as the final level of concept achievement. Minimum score is 8 at the rate of one per category per set.

What is Intelligence?

According to David Wechsler (1975) — Intelligence is the capacity to understand the world, think rationally and use resources effectively when faced with challenges. Mental Tests first employed by Cattell in 1870. Intelligence testing movement launched in 1905 by Alfred Binet. William Stern devised IQ = MA/CA × 100.

Intelligence tests: (1) Verbal, Nonverbal & Performance; (2) Individual & Group; (3) Speed & Power.

Who developed WAPIS and what is it? EXP 5
WAPIS is the performance scale of WAIS adapted to Indian conditions by Prabha Ramalingaswamy. 5 sub-tests: Picture Completion, Digit Symbol, Block Design, Picture Arrangement, Object Assembly. Individual test used in clinical studies and counselling.
When is the Block Design test discontinued? EXP 5
After 3 consecutive failures. Failure on both trials of either design 1 or 2 is considered one failure.

STATSThe Median Test▾
What it is

The Median Test is used to compare the performance of two independent groups. A common median is found. If the two groups have been drawn at random from the same population, ½ of the scores in each group should lie above and ½ below the common median. To test this null hypothesis, we draw up a 2 × 2 table and calculate X² (Chi-square).

Steps to Calculate
  1. Combine all scores from both groups and arrange in ascending order.
  2. Calculate the Common Median:
    Mdn = N + 1 2
  3. For each group, count scores above and below the common median.
  4. Enter frequencies into a 2 × 2 fold table.
  5. Calculate X² using the formula below.
  6. Calculate df = (r−1)(c−1) = 1 for a 2×2 table.
  7. Enter Table E with df = 1. At 0.05 level = 3.84; at 0.01 level = 6.63.
  8. If obtained X² > table value → reject null hypothesis.
Formula
Chi-Square for Median Test
X² = N · |AD − BC|² (A+B)(C+D)(A+C)(B+D)
Yate's Correction — used when any box entry is 5 or less:

X² = N · (|AD − BC| − N/2)² (A+B)(C+D)(A+C)(B+D)
Interpretation
Obtained X² > table value → Null hypothesis rejected — significant difference between groups.
Obtained X² < table value → Null hypothesis retained — no significant difference.
df = 1 → 0.05 level = 3.84  ·  0.01 level = 6.63
Worked Example

A clinical psychologist wants to investigate the effects of a tranquilizer on hand tremor. 14 patients are given the drug (experimental group) and 14 patients are given the Placebo (control group). Tremor is measured by a steadiness tester. Does the drug increase steadiness (indicated by a lower score in the experimental group)?

N = 28 total scores.
Mdn = 28 + 1 2 = 14.5 → 14th score = 52, 15th score = 54 → Common Median = 53

PROBLEM To measure the manual dexterity of the subject by administering MRMT. To determine experimentally the effect of competition on the performance of the subject. To study experimentally the effect of perception of size on judgement of weights. To study the processes of Concept Achievement using the CAT by M. Rajamanickam. To assess the intelligence of the subject using WAPIS.
HYPOTHESIS No hypothesis — aptitude / norm-referenced test. Performance of the subject significantly improves with competition. H1: Perception of larger size leads to underestimation of weight.
H2: Perception of smaller size leads to overestimation of weight. 		H1: Correct answers progressively increase in each set as concept achievement takes place.
